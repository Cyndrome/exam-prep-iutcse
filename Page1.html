Question 1 Explain the Evolution of Islamic Historiography, mentioning the popular Classic Muslim Historians — Ibn Hisham, Ibn Qutaiba and Tabari.
Answer

Islamic historiography did not appear suddenly; it evolved in stages out of pre-Islamic oral tradition, was given direction and urgency by the Qur'an's own treatment of history, and matured through successive generations of scholar-historians into a rigorous, source-critical discipline.

1. A Religious Foundation for History

Islam's strong ‘historical consciousness’ — a phrase Bernard Lewis uses — is rooted in the Qur'an itself, which presents nature, history and intuition as the three channels of human knowledge. As the lecture notes, the Qur'an states that the stories of earlier prophets and nations are narrated “to strengthen your heart” (Qur'an 11:120). This is reinforced elsewhere in the Qur'an: Surah Yusuf declares, “There was certainly in their stories a lesson for those of understanding” (12:111), and Surah Al-Imran instructs believers to “proceed through the earth and observe how was the end of those who denied” (3:137). History, in this worldview, is not nostalgia but moral instruction — a pattern the Qur'an applies to 'Ad, Thamud, the people of Noah, the Israelites and the people of Sodom and Gomorrah.

2. Pre-Islamic Roots

Before Islam, the Arabs already possessed a rudimentary historical tradition in the Ayyam al-'Arab (the ‘Battle-Days of the Arabs’) and tribal genealogies preserved in poetry. Bernard Lewis judges this material largely mythological, yet valuable because of its closeness to real life and its interest in real people and events — qualities that made it usable as raw material for later historiography. Franz Rosenthal adds that “history loomed very large in Muhammad's thinking,” while Prof. Gibb credits the Qur'an with making the Arabs genuinely ‘history-conscious.’

3. Early Recording: the Maghazi and the Umayyad Court

With Islam, the poetic impulse to record heroic deeds was redirected toward the life and campaigns of the Prophet (PBUH). Tribal pride in poetry persisted into the Umayyad period, and Caliph Muawiyah himself summoned Abid bin Shariyya to record the history of the kings of Yaman — early evidence that historical writing was being formally patronised. Alongside this grew Maghazi literature: systematic records of the Prophet's expeditions (their aims, leaders, participants and results). Because the Qur'an mentions only a few campaigns, this material had to be gathered by questioning large numbers of people — work undertaken chiefly by Ibn Ishaq and al-Waqidi.

4. Ibn Ishaq, and the Refinement by Ibn Hisham

Muhammad Ibn Ishaq (d. 767 C.E.) is regarded as the foremost authority on Sirah and Maghazi, rivalled only by al-Waqidi. He authored Sirat Rasul Allah — the first biography of the Prophet commissioned under the Abbasids, by Caliph al-Mansur. His work, however, survives today mainly through the recension of his student, Ibn Hisham (d. 213/218 A.H.). According to Khuda Bakhsh and Prof. Abd al-Aziz al-Duri, Ibn Hisham represented a genuine methodological advance: he corrected unreliable reports (riwayat) in Ibn Ishaq's text, omitted fabricated poetry, and aligned his approach with that of the Muhaddithin (hadith scholars) — that is, he applied isnad (chain-of-narration) criticism to historical material, the same source-verification method that hadith scholars such as Imam Bukhari and Imam Muslim were systematising for prophetic traditions. This cross-fertilisation between hadith methodology and history-writing is one of the most distinctive features of Islamic historiography.

5. Ibn Qutaiba: Widening the Scope

Ibn Qutaiba (d. 276 A.H. / 889 C.E.) authored Kitab al-Ma'arif (‘The Book of Knowledge’), which moved historiography beyond pure Sirah and Maghazi. It opens with an account of creation (drawing on Biblical and Arab legend), proceeds through the patriarchs, pre-Islamic Arab tribal genealogy, the biography of the Prophet, his Companions and the caliphs down to Ibn Qutaiba's own time, and closes with the southern Arabian dynasties and pre-Islamic Persian kings. This represents a transition from narrow traditionist narrative toward a broader intellectual vision incorporating genealogy, geography and ethnography.

6. Al-Tabari: the Golden Age

Al-Tabari (d. 310 A.H. / 923 C.E.) represents the culmination of this evolution. According to Margoliouth, he was the first major Muslim historian to apply a comprehensive, analytical method organised strictly around chronology. His Tarikh al-Rusul wa al-Muluk (History of Prophets and Kings) runs from creation through the Prophet's life, the early caliphs and the Umayyad and Abbasid dynasties up to 302 A.H. / 915 C.E. — the surviving text being, by report, only a tenth of his original draft. Tabari preserved isnad for nearly every report he transmitted, making his work both encyclopedic and verifiable, and it is this combination of scale and method that marks the ‘Golden Age’ of Muslim historiography.

Conclusion

Islamic historiography therefore evolved through clearly traceable stages: from semi-mythological tribal poetry, through Qur'an-inspired moral narration, to isnad-based Sirah/Maghazi writing (Ibn Ishaq, refined by Ibn Hisham), to the encyclopedic broadening of Ibn Qutaiba, and finally to the rigorous, chronologically organised Golden Age represented by Tabari — after which Masudi's thematic-geographic method and Ibn Khaldun's sociological philosophy of history carried the discipline to its classical peak.

Question 2 Write down the Evolution of Islamic Historiography, mentioning the popular Classic Muslim Historians briefly.
Answer

In brief, the evolution of Islamic historiography may be summarised as follows:

  • Religious foundation: The Qur'an's strong historical consciousness — narrating the stories of past nations “to strengthen your heart” (Qur'an 11:120) and as “a lesson for those of understanding” (Qur'an 12:111) — gave Muslims a religious motive to preserve and learn from the past.
  • Pre-Islamic roots: The Ayyam al-'Arab (battle-days of the Arabs) and tribal genealogies preserved in poetry formed a semi-mythological but useful narrative foundation (Bernard Lewis).
  • Umayyad-era patronage: Caliph Muawiyah commissioned Abid bin Shariyya to record the history of the Kings of Yaman.
  • Maghazi literature: Systematic records of the Prophet's expeditions, developed chiefly by Ibn Ishaq and al-Waqidi.
  • Ibn Ishaq (d. 767 C.E.): Authored Sirat Rasul Allah, the first biography of the Prophet, commissioned by Caliph al-Mansur.
  • Ibn Hisham (d. 213/218 A.H.): Refined and preserved Ibn Ishaq's Sirah, removing fabricated poetry and applying hadith-style isnad criticism.
  • Ibn Qutaiba (d. 889 C.E.): His Kitab al-Ma'arif widened history to cosmology, genealogy, caliphal biography and pre-Islamic dynasties.
  • Al-Tabari (d. 923 C.E.): His Tarikh al-Rusul wa al-Muluk represents the Golden Age — the first comprehensive, strictly chronological, isnad-based universal history.
  • Al-Masudi (d. 956 C.E.): Introduced a thematic, encyclopedic, geographic method in Muruj al-Dhahab.
  • Ibn Khaldun (d. 1406 C.E.): His Muqaddimah turned history into a philosophical-sociological science, explaining the rise and fall of states through asabiyyah (group solidarity).

Sources: Lecture slides 5–27; Qur'an 11:120, 12:111; Bernard Lewis; Franz Rosenthal; Abd al-Aziz al-Duri.

Question 3 Describe the development of Islamic historiography, including a brief overview of prominent classical Muslim historians.
Answer

The development of Islamic historiography passed through three broad phases:

  • Formative phase — moral and oral history: Pre-Islamic poetic memory (Ayyam al-'Arab) combined with the Qur'an's narrative use of history to create an early Muslim interest in recording the past as instruction rather than mere chronicle.
  • Biographical-traditionist phase — Sirah and Maghazi: Scholars collected, cross-checked and recorded the life and campaigns of the Prophet using methods borrowed from hadith criticism (isnad). This phase produced Ibn Ishaq, Ibn Hisham, Ibn Sa'd and al-Waqidi.
  • Analytical and philosophical phase: Historians widened their scope from Sirah to universal, chronological and finally sociological history — represented by Ibn Qutaiba, al-Tabari, al-Masudi and, ultimately, Ibn Khaldun, whose Muqaddimah founded a new philosophical discipline of history.

Question 4 Assess Ibn Khaldun's contributions to historiography based on the observations of De Boer and Walter J. Fischel.
Answer

De Boer's observation: De Boer remarks that Ibn Khaldun “comes forward with a claim to establish a new philosophical discipline, of which Aristotle had no conception.” This is a strong assessment: it places Ibn Khaldun not merely as a continuator of earlier Muslim historians but as the founder of an entirely new branch of knowledge — what is now recognised as sociology and the philosophy of history. Unlike Aristotle, who theorised about politics and ethics in the abstract, Ibn Khaldun grounded his theory in the observable, recurring patterns of how human societies form, cohere and collapse.

Walter J. Fischel's observation: Fischel notes that Ibn Khaldun regarded al-Tabari as one of the few ‘good’ historians — among those “not more than the fingers on one hand.” This shows two things. First, Ibn Khaldun was a discerning critic of the historiographical tradition that preceded him, not an uncritical compiler; he selected and trusted only a handful of earlier historians. Second, he drew heavily on Tabari's material — especially for the history of non-Arab peoples — incorporating it into his own work while reshaping it around his socio-philosophical framework. The lecture also notes that al-Masudi, unlike Tabari, had already begun moving away from the strict traditionist (isnad-by-isnad) school toward thematic interpretation; Ibn Khaldun extended this departure much further, subordinating individual reports to an overarching causal theory of history.

Assessment of his contribution: Taken together, De Boer's and Fischel's observations point to the same conclusion from two directions. De Boer assesses the originality of Ibn Khaldun's discipline — a wholly new philosophical science with no Aristotelian precedent. Fischel assesses his relationship to the historiographical tradition — selective, critical use of predecessors such as Tabari, combined with rejection of mere chronicling. Ibn Khaldun's achievement was therefore to absorb the accumulated factual material of earlier Muslim historiography (Tabari's chronology, Masudi's thematic breadth) and reorganise it around the causal question of why civilisations rise and fall — a transformation that justifies his later recognition (outside the lecture's direct citations, but widely affirmed in Islamic intellectual history) as a forerunner of modern sociology and the philosophy of history.

Sources: Lecture slide 23; W. J. Fischel, Ibn Khaldun and Tamerlane (University of California Press, 1952).

Question 5 Why are Ibn Khaldun's contributions as a historian praiseworthy? What do you know about the 'Muqaddima Ibn Khaldun', the theory of Group Mind, and the Philosophy of Nation State?
Answer

Why Ibn Khaldun's contribution is praiseworthy

Ibn Khaldun's contribution is praiseworthy chiefly because he transformed history from a simple chronicle of events into a systematic discipline aimed at understanding the causes behind the rise and decline of civilisations. Where earlier historians such as Tabari focused on accurate, chronologically ordered transmission of reports, and Masudi added thematic and geographic breadth, Ibn Khaldun went further: he asked why states and civilisations behave as they do, and answered with a coherent causal theory grounded in social cohesion, economics and political structure. This made history, in his hands, a critical and analytical science rather than mere fact-collection or storytelling — the foundation, in the lecture's words, of both sociology and modern historiography.

The Muqaddimah

The Muqaddimah (‘Prolegomena’/Introduction) is the lengthy preface to Ibn Khaldun's larger work, Kitab al-Ibar, forming its first volume. In it, Ibn Khaldun lays out his sociological and philosophical perspective on history — examining the dynamics of state formation, tribal cohesion (asabiyyah), and the economic and social structures that drive the rise and fall of civilisations. The second book of the four-volume Kitab al-Ibar then applies this framework to a universal history of ancient nations, including the pre-Islamic Arabs, Babylonians, Nabateans and Copts, reflecting Ibn Khaldun's commitment to writing history on a genuinely universal scale.

The Theory of Group Mind (Asabiyyah)

Ibn Khaldun identifies group solidarity, or 'Asabiyyah' (sense of oneness / ‘Group Mind’), together with religion, as the two principal factors in the formation of a state. According to Khuda Bakhsh, Ibn Khaldun did not, however, treat religion as indispensable to civilisation-building in the way he treated asabiyyah — group cohesion could, on its own, generate a state. In this respect, as the lecture notes, Ibn Khaldun anticipated by roughly five centuries an intellectual tendency that emerged in Western thought only much later. Franz Rosenthal observes that religious fervour and the appearance of prophets can intensify and accelerate political movements — though even prophets, he notes, cannot succeed in the world without concrete political support — and cites the extraordinary, ‘superhuman’ success of Islam itself as history's foremost example of this. The Qur'an's own treatment of nations reinforces this cyclical, conditional view of a people's fortunes: “And for every nation is a [specified] term; when their time has come, they will not remain behind an hour, nor will they precede [it]” (Qur'an 7:34) — a statement consonant with Ibn Khaldun's view that every civilisation has a natural, time-bound life-cycle. In modern times, the lecture notes that Group Mind or unity of purpose, even without a shared religion, has been the decisive factor in forming heterogeneous states such as Switzerland, Belgium, Canada and the United States.

The Philosophy of Nation State: Stages of Civilisation

Ibn Khaldun further argued that human civilisation passes through definite stages: from pastoral and nomadic life, to urban and city life, and ultimately to vast imperial dominion. The simple, rugged vitality of nomadic life is gradually replaced by opulence and luxury; this breeds indolence in place of earlier activity, which in turn produces an effeminate character unfit for self-defence. At this stage the ruler is forced to rely on mercenaries to protect the state, and the state itself does not long survive — it eventually dies a natural death. Alternatively, the cycle may be broken when the ruler's autocracy is challenged by a popular movement; the state may then regain strength and vitality, and the entire cycle begins again. This cyclical theory of the birth, maturity, decline and renewal of states is the practical, political expression of his more abstract theory of asabiyyah, and together they constitute Ibn Khaldun's distinctive ‘philosophy of the nation state.’
