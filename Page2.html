PART I — Birth, Family & Early Life

Question 1

Q1. Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) was born in the Year of the Elephant. According to Sahih Muslim (#1162), why did he choose to fast every Monday? State the exact reason he himself gave.

Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) was asked about fasting on Mondays. He replied:

“That is the day I was born and the day Revelation began.” — Sahih Muslim #1162

He therefore chose to observe voluntary fasting on Mondays as a form of gratitude to Allah (SWT) for two of the greatest divine blessings in his life: his birth into this world and the commencement of Quranic revelation.

The Prophet (PBUH) was born on 12 Rabi' al-Awwal in the Year of the Elephant (c. 570 CE), corresponding approximately to 20 April 571 CE, which was a Monday.

Monday fasting therefore commemorates both his birth and the beginning of divine revelation.

Question 2

Q2. Name the Prophet's (PBUH) nursing mother and the tribe she belonged to. At what age did the first miraculous Shaqq al-Sadr occur? State two significant events between ages 6 and 8.

The Prophet's (PBUH) nursing mother was Halimah al-Sa'diyyah (RA), from the tribe of Banu Sa'd.

The first Shaqq al-Sadr (Chest Opening) occurred when he was approximately four years old while living among Banu Sa'd. Angel Jibril (AS) opened his chest and purified his heart in preparation for prophethood.

Two significant events between ages 6 and 8:

  • Age 6 – Death of his mother Aminah bint Wahb at Abwa. He then came under the care of his grandfather Abdul-Muttalib.
  • Age 8 – Death of Abdul-Muttalib, after which Abu Talib became his guardian.

These early losses shaped his lifelong compassion toward orphans and the vulnerable.

Question 3

Q3. At age 35, before prophethood, the Prophet (PBUH) resolved the Hajar al-Aswad dispute. Describe: (a) the dispute, (b) his solution, (c) what it reveals about his leadership.

(a) Nature of the Dispute

During reconstruction of the Ka'bah after flood damage, the Quraysh tribes disagreed over who should have the honour of placing the Black Stone back into its position. The disagreement nearly escalated into bloodshed.

(b) His Solution

Muhammad (PBUH) spread a cloak on the ground, placed the Black Stone in its centre, and invited representatives from every tribe to lift the cloth together. Once raised to the correct height, he personally placed the stone into position.

(c) Leadership Qualities Revealed

  • Impartiality and fairness
  • Wisdom under pressure
  • Creative conflict resolution
  • Structural thinking
  • Public trust as Al-Amin

The incident demonstrates his wisdom and integrity years before receiving revelation.

Knowledge and learning were therefore established as foundational themes of Islam from its very first revelation.

PART III — Dawah, Persecution & Hijrah

Question 6

Q6. Identify the first five people to accept Islam (Al-Sabiqun al-Awwalun). Mention each person's relationship to the Prophet (PBUH). Which Hadith collection and volume documents this in Kitab al-Maghazi?

The earliest believers in Islam are known as Al-Sabiqun al-Awwalun (the Foremost Early Muslims).

  • Khadijah bint Khuwaylid (RA) – Wife of the Prophet (PBUH) and the very first person to accept Islam.
  • Ali ibn Abi Talib (RA) – Cousin of the Prophet (PBUH), raised in his household and among the earliest believers.
  • Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (RA) – Close friend and companion who immediately accepted Islam upon invitation.
  • Zayd ibn Harithah (RA) – Freed slave and adopted son who lived in the Prophet's household.

Some historical sources additionally include Uthman ibn Affan (RA) or Bilal ibn Rabah (RA) among the earliest converts.

These events are documented in Sahih al-Bukhari, Kitab al-Maghazi, Volume 5 (Hadith #3949–4473), with complementary references in Sirat Ibn Hisham and Tabaqat Ibn Sa'd.

Question 7

Q7. Analyse the five stages of Quraysh's persecution of early Muslims. Provide one example for each stage. Then explain the two major losses of the Year of Sorrow and how the Prophet (PBUH) continued his mission.

Five Stages of Qurayshi Persecution

  • Stage 1 – Verbal abuse and ridicule: The Quraysh mocked the Prophet (PBUH), calling him a poet, sorcerer and madman. Abu Jahl became one of the leading figures in this campaign.
  • Stage 2 – Physical torture: Bilal ibn Rabah (RA) was tortured under the desert sun with a heavy stone on his chest. Sumayyah bint Khabbat (RA) became the first martyr of Islam after being killed by Abu Jahl.
  • Stage 3 – Economic boycott: Banu Hashim and Banu Muttalib were subjected to a severe boycott and confined to Shi'b Abi Talib for approximately three years.
  • Stage 4 – Social and psychological pressure: Many Muslims migrated to Abyssinia under the protection of King Negus to escape persecution.
  • Stage 5 – Assassination plot: The Quraysh planned the collective assassination of the Prophet (PBUH), leading directly to the Hijrah to Madinah.

The Year of Sorrow

The Prophet (PBUH) experienced two devastating personal losses:

  • Death of Khadijah (RA), his beloved wife and greatest supporter.
  • Death of Abu Talib, his uncle and principal tribal protector.

Despite these losses, the Prophet (PBUH) continued his mission by travelling to Ta'if, enduring hardship with patience, and later calling the tribes visiting Makkah during Hajj. His efforts ultimately led to the support of the Ansar and the Hijrah to Madinah.

Question 8

Q8. Explain the Hijrah to Madinah by stating: (a) why the Prophet (PBUH) left, (b) with whom, (c) where they hid, (d) its historical significance.
  • (a) Reason: Allah revealed the Quraysh assassination plot and commanded the Hijrah. The migration also took place because the Ansar of Madinah had accepted Islam and offered protection.
  • (b) Companion: Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (RA).
  • (c) Hiding place: The Cave of Thawr (Ghar Thawr), where they remained for three days.
  • (d) Historical significance: The Hijrah marks the beginning of the Islamic Hijri Calendar (AH) and the establishment of the first organised Muslim community and state.

Ali ibn Abi Talib (RA) remained behind temporarily to return trust deposits entrusted to the Prophet (PBUH), demonstrating his continued trustworthiness even in the face of danger.

Question 9

Q9. What was the Charter of Madinah, and why is it described as the world's first written constitution? State three rights or obligations it guaranteed.

The Charter of Madinah (Sahifat al-Madinah) was a written agreement establishing the political and social framework of the newly formed community in Madinah.

It governed relations among Muslims, Jewish tribes and other communities, defining their rights, obligations and mechanisms for cooperation.

It is widely regarded as the world's first written constitution because it formally codified governmental authority, legal principles and collective responsibilities in a written document.

Three Rights and Obligations

  • Religious freedom for Muslims and Jews alike.
  • Collective defence of Madinah against external aggression.
  • Major disputes to be referred to Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) for adjudication.

Additional guarantees included protection of the weak and prohibition of treachery, establishing one of history's earliest multi-religious constitutional systems.

PART IV — Kitab al-Maghazi: The Major Battles

Question 10

Q10. The Battle of Badr (2 AH) is called Yawm al-Furqan. Explain: (a) why, (b) the force ratio, (c) the meaning of Quran 8:17, and cite the relevant Hadith reference.

(a) Why it is called Yawm al-Furqan

The Battle of Badr is known as the Day of Distinction because it clearly distinguished truth from falsehood and established the political legitimacy and divine support of the emerging Muslim state.

(b) Force Ratio

Approximately 313 Muslims faced nearly 1,000 Quraysh fighters, representing roughly a 1:3 numerical disadvantage.

The Quraysh possessed superior equipment, cavalry and armour, while the Muslims had very limited resources.

(c) Quran 8:17

The verse declares:

"It was not you who slew them, but Allah who slew them."

This teaches that victory ultimately came through divine support rather than human strength alone, emphasizing humility and reliance upon Allah.

The Battle of Badr is documented in Sahih al-Bukhari, Kitab al-Maghazi, Hadith #3956–3988.

Seventy Quraysh leaders were killed, seventy captured, and the victory permanently shifted the balance of power in Arabia.

PART IV — Kitab al-Maghazi: The Major Battles

Question 11

Q11. The Battle of Uhud (3 AH) is described as "a profound lesson in discipline." Explain: (a) the command the archers disobeyed and why, (b) who exploited the mistake, (c) the number of Muslim martyrs and the most notable among them, (d) the Quranic verse addressing the event and its message.

(a) The Command that was Disobeyed

Before the Battle of Uhud, the Prophet (PBUH) stationed fifty archers on Jabal al-Rumah (Archers' Hill) with a clear command:

"Do not leave your positions regardless of whether we are winning or losing."

When the Quraysh appeared to retreat and spoils became visible on the battlefield, the majority of the archers assumed victory had already been secured and abandoned their assigned positions to collect the spoils.

Only a small number remained at their posts, directly violating the Prophet's command.

(b) Who Exploited the Mistake?

Khalid ibn al-Walid (RA), then serving as a commander of the Quraysh, immediately recognised the exposed flank and led his cavalry around the hill, launching a devastating rear attack that reversed the course of the battle.

(c) Muslim Casualties

Approximately seventy Muslims were martyred.

Among the most notable was Hamza ibn Abdul-Muttalib (RA), the beloved uncle of the Prophet (PBUH), known as Asadullah (Lion of Allah).

The Prophet (PBUH) himself was wounded, lost a tooth, and rumours of his death briefly spread among the Muslim army.

(d) Quranic Reference

Surah Aal-e-Imran (3:152) addresses this event:

"...some of you desired the world, and then He diverted you from them to test you..."

The verse teaches that obedience, sincerity and steadfastness are essential conditions for victory, and that worldly distractions can lead to painful consequences.

Question 12

Q12. Compare and contrast the Battle of Khandaq (5 AH) and the Conquest of Makkah (8 AH). For each event state the year, force sizes, tactical innovation and final outcome. What common principle do they reveal about the Prophet's (PBUH) approach to conflict resolution?

Battle of Khandaq (The Trench)

  • Year: 5 AH / 627 CE
  • Force Size: Approximately 10,000 Confederate forces against roughly 3,000 Muslim defenders.
  • Tactical Innovation: On the suggestion of Salman al-Farisi (RA), a defensive trench (khandaq) was dug around the exposed side of Madinah. This was an unprecedented military strategy in Arabia and rendered enemy cavalry ineffective.
  • Outcome: After approximately twenty-seven days of unsuccessful siege, severe weather and divine intervention forced the Confederates to withdraw, ending Quraysh's offensive dominance.

Conquest of Makkah (Fath Makkah)

  • Year: 8 AH / 630 CE
  • Force Size: Approximately 10,000 Muslim soldiers entered Makkah while facing virtually no organised resistance.
  • Tactical Innovation: The city was entered simultaneously from multiple directions with strict orders to avoid unnecessary fighting and to engage only when attacked.
  • Outcome: Makkah surrendered peacefully. The Prophet (PBUH) declared a general amnesty, purified the Ka'bah of idols and initiated widespread reconciliation rather than revenge.

Consistent Principle Revealed

Both events demonstrate the Prophet's preference for strategic wisdom, minimum necessary force and peaceful resolution whenever possible.

Even after complete victory, he consistently favoured mercy, reconciliation and long-term stability over vengeance and destruction.

Question 13

Q13. The Treaty of Hudaybiyyah appeared to be a defeat for Muslims, yet Allah called it "A Clear Victory." State three seemingly unfavourable terms and explain two reasons why it became a strategic triumph.

Three Seemingly Unfavourable Terms

  • Muslims would return to Madinah without performing Umrah that year.
  • Any Qurayshi who accepted Islam and fled to Madinah would be returned, whereas Muslims leaving Islam and joining Quraysh would not be returned.
  • A ten-year ceasefire restricted military engagement between both parties.

Why It Became a Strategic Victory

  • The period of peace allowed Islam to spread more rapidly than during years of warfare. Trade and communication resumed, and many important leaders—including Khalid ibn al-Walid (RA) and Amr ibn al-As (RA)— accepted Islam.
  • The treaty enabled the Prophet (PBUH) to send diplomatic letters to major world rulers. When Quraysh later violated the treaty, the Muslims gained complete moral and political justification for the peaceful Conquest of Makkah.

Thus, what initially appeared to be a diplomatic concession ultimately became one of the greatest strategic victories in Islamic history.

Q15. Identify all seven children of Prophet Muhammad (PBUH), stating their mother and brief fate. Then explain the significance of Quran 33:40.

Sons

  • Al-Qasim – Mother: Khadijah (RA). Died in infancy.
  • Abdullah (al-Tayyib / al-Tahir) – Mother: Khadijah (RA). Died in infancy.
  • Ibrahim – Mother: Mariyah al-Qibtiyyah (RA). Died in early childhood in Madinah.

Daughters

  • Zaynab (RA) – Mother: Khadijah (RA). Married Abul-As ibn Rabi' and later died in Madinah.
  • Ruqayyah (RA) – Mother: Khadijah (RA). Wife of Uthman ibn Affan (RA); died during the time of Badr.
  • Umm Kulthoom (RA) – Mother: Khadijah (RA). Married Uthman (RA) after Ruqayyah's death.
  • Fatimah al-Zahra (RA) – Mother: Khadijah (RA). Married Ali ibn Abi Talib (RA), mother of Hasan and Husayn (RA), and died about six months after the Prophet (PBUH).

Theological Significance of Quran 33:40

The verse states:

"Muhammad is not the father of any of your men, but the Messenger of Allah and the Seal of the Prophets."

It establishes the finality of prophethood (Khatm al-Nubuwwah) and prevents any future claim to prophetic authority through biological descent.

It also corrected pre-Islamic concepts of adoption by clarifying that an adopted son is not equivalent to a biological son in matters of legal lineage and marriage.

PART VI — Farewell Pilgrimage & Last Sermon

Question 16

Q16. "The Farewell Sermon was the world's first universal declaration of human rights." Critically evaluate this claim by: (a) stating four key messages with Quranic evidence, (b) comparing one message with modern international law, (c) explaining the revelation of Quran 5:3 and its theological significance.

The Farewell Sermon (Khutbat al-Wida') was delivered on 9 Dhul-Hijjah, 10 AH (632 CE) at the Plain of Arafat before more than one hundred thousand companions. It summarized the ethical, legal and spiritual foundations of Islam during the Prophet's final pilgrimage.

(a) Four Key Messages with Quranic Evidence

  • Sanctity of Human Life and Property
    The Prophet (PBUH) declared that people's lives, wealth and honour are sacred.
    Quran: Surah Al-Ma'idah (5:32).
  • Equality of Humanity
    No Arab is superior to a non-Arab and no race is superior to another except through righteousness.
    Quran: Surah Al-Hujurat (49:13).
  • Rights of Women
    The Prophet (PBUH) emphasized kindness, fairness and mutual rights between husbands and wives.
    Quran: Surah Al-Baqarah (2:228).
  • Prohibition of Riba (Usury)
    Economic exploitation through interest was abolished.
    Quran: Surah Al-Baqarah (2:275).

(b) Comparison with Modern International Law

The Sermon's declaration of racial equality closely parallels Article 1 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, which states that all human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights.

While the UDHR derives authority from international consensus, the Farewell Sermon grounds equality in divine revelation and the shared origin of humanity before Allah.

(c) Revelation of Quran 5:3

"Today I have perfected your religion for you, completed My favour upon you, and have approved Islam as your religion."

This verse signifies:

  • The completion of Islamic legislation and guidance.
  • The perfection of the religion as a complete way of life.
  • The finality of divine revelation and the completion of the Prophet's mission.

Although parallels can be drawn with modern human rights documents, the Farewell Sermon remains fundamentally a divinely revealed moral and spiritual framework rather than a political treaty.

PART VII — Character, Civilization & Legacy

Question 17

Q17. Name the two titles given to Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) before prophethood, explain their meanings and their significance.

Before revelation, the people of Quraysh themselves honoured Muhammad (PBUH) with two remarkable titles:

  • Al-Amin (The Trustworthy)
    He was universally known for honesty, reliability and faithfully returning trusts placed in his care.
  • Al-Sadiq (The Truthful)
    He was known for absolute truthfulness and never being known to lie or deceive.

These titles are significant because they were bestowed by his own society—including many who later opposed him—and therefore constitute powerful testimony to his integrity before prophethood.

His lifelong reputation for honesty strongly supports the credibility of his prophetic claim.

Question 18

Q18. Aisha (RA) said: "His character was the Quran." Describe four core character traits of the Prophet (PBUH), provide supporting evidence, and explain the relevance of one trait to professional engineering ethics.

Four Core Character Traits

  • Mercy (Rahmah)
    Allah described him as "a mercy to all worlds." (Surah Al-Anbiya 21:107).
  • Justice (Adalah)
    He declared that even his own daughter would not be exempt from justice if she committed wrongdoing.
  • Wisdom (Hikmah)
    His leadership in resolving disputes and establishing the Charter of Madinah exemplified practical wisdom.
  • Honesty and Integrity (Sidq and Amanah)
    His life embodied truthfulness and trustworthiness, reflected in his titles Al-Amin and Al-Sadiq and reinforced by Quran 33:21.

Engineering Ethics

Justice and trustworthiness are fundamental principles of engineering. Engineers must honestly report data, avoid falsification, prioritize public safety and remain ethical even under pressure.

The Prophet's unwavering commitment to fairness provides a timeless model for responsible professional conduct.

Question 19

Q19. The Prophet (PBUH) said: "The best of you is the one who is best to his family." Describe four examples of exemplary family conduct and explain their relevance for Muslim engineering students.

Examples of Family Conduct

  • He actively helped with household chores, including mending clothes and assisting family members.
  • He displayed affection and kindness toward his wives and children, playing with them and showing emotional warmth.
  • He treated his wives fairly and consulted them on important matters, including at Hudaybiyyah.
  • He honoured and deeply loved his daughter Fatimah (RA), standing when she entered and treating her with exceptional respect.

Practical Ethical Model

The Prophetic example teaches that greatness in public leadership must be matched by excellence in private conduct.

Engineering students should cultivate honesty, service, patience, respect and compassion within their families, since these same qualities form the basis of ethical professional behaviour and responsible leadership.

Question 20

Q20. Michael H. Hart ranked Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) first among the 100 most influential persons in history. Justify this ranking using multiple domains, one non-Muslim quotation, one Quranic reference and one authenticated Hadith.

Governance

The Charter of Madinah established one of history's earliest written constitutional systems governing a multi-faith society through defined rights and responsibilities.

Social Reform

The Prophet (PBUH) transformed Arabian society by combating tribalism, protecting women's rights, promoting justice and establishing the principle that honour depends upon righteousness rather than lineage.

Quran: Surah Al-Hujurat (49:13):

"Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you."

Knowledge and Education

The emphasis on literacy, education and scholarship laid the foundation for later scientific and intellectual achievements of Islamic civilization.

After Badr, literate prisoners were allowed to earn their freedom by teaching Muslims to read and write.

Hadith Reference: Sahih al-Bukhari, Kitab al-Maghazi (#3956 and related narrations).

Non-Muslim Testimony

"My choice of Muhammad to lead the list of the world's most influential persons may surprise some readers, but he was the only man in history who was supremely successful on both the religious and secular level." — Michael H. Hart, The 100 (1978)

Question 21

Q21. "Loving the Prophet (PBUH) is not an emotion — it is a practice." Using the six practical ways to love the Prophet (PBUH) taught in the lecture, construct a structured argument showing how each practice can be meaningfully applied in the daily life of a student at IUT. Your answer MUST: (a) reference Quran 33:56 (on Salawat) and Quran 3:31 (on following the Prophet), (b) connect at least TWO of the six practices specifically to professional engineering ethics, and (c) cite the Tirmidhi Hadith on Sunnah revival that is directly quoted in the lecture slides.

The lecture quotes the Prophet (PBUH) directly: 'Whoever revives my Sunnah then he has loved me. And whoever loved me, he shall be with me in Paradise.' — Tirmidhi. This Hadith is the theological foundation of the argument: love for the Prophet (PBUH) is not passive feeling, not mere sentiment, but active, embodied practice. It is measured by conduct, not emotion alone. For a student at IUT, this takes the following concrete form across six practices:

1. Praying the Five Daily Prayers and Reviving Sunnah

Salah is the most direct act of following the Prophet (PBUH). IUT students are in an environment of tight schedules, lab sessions, and academic pressure — yet the Prophet (PBUH) himself managed to lead armies, run a state, and never miss a prayer. Reviving Sunnah prayers (such as two rak'ahs before Fajr or four before Zuhr) demonstrates that love for the Prophet (PBUH) takes priority over convenience. Quran 3:31 states: 'Say: If you love Allah, then follow me — Allah will love you and forgive your sins.' Obedience to the Sunnah is the Quranic test of genuine love.

2. Studying the Seerah (Biography of the Prophet)

An IUT student who spends time studying Sirat books — even thirty minutes weekly — knows the Prophet (PBUH) not as a distant historical figure but as a personal model. Knowing how he handled the stress of the Battle of Khandaq (fasting, praying in shifts, leading under siege) gives the student a framework for managing academic pressure with patience and trust in Allah. One cannot genuinely love what one does not know.

3. Sending Salawat (Blessings upon the Prophet)

Quran 33:56 states: 'Indeed, Allah and His angels send blessings upon the Prophet. O you who have believed, ask Allah to confer blessing upon him.' Salawat can be recited between lectures, while walking between labs, or before exams. It is the most accessible and portable act of love — requiring no equipment, no timetable slot, and no silence. For an engineering student, integrating Salawat into the transitions of the day is both spiritually enriching and a constant mindfulness of prophetic guidance.

4. Giving to the Poor and Practising Generosity

The Prophet (PBUH) never turned away a beggar. On the IUT campus and in Dhaka, poverty and need are visible realities. A student who donates a portion of pocket money to a classmate in need, or who participates in community service, is reviving a direct Sunnah. The Prophetic model of economic justice — banning exploitation, protecting the poor through Zakat, sharing surplus — provides a moral vocabulary for an engineering student thinking about the societal impact of the technology they build.

Connection to Professional Engineering Ethics (Practices 4 and 1 above):

Practice 4 — Generosity and concern for the poor — connects directly to engineering ethics through the concept of socially responsible design. Engineers who follow the Prophetic model ask not only 'does this work?' but 'who does this serve, and who does this harm?' The Prophet (PBUH) released literate prisoners in exchange for teaching Muslims to read — prioritising human development over military advantage. An engineer who applies the same principle would prioritise accessibility, safety of vulnerable populations, and equitable distribution of technological benefit in their professional work.

Practice 1 — Following Sunnah through prayer and discipline — connects to professional ethics through integrity under pressure. The Prophet (PBUH) maintained his five daily prayers even in the most extreme circumstances (including active battle). An engineering professional who maintains ethical boundaries even under commercial, hierarchical, or deadline pressure is reviving this Sunnah of principled steadfastness in the face of difficulty.

5. Adopting the Prophet's Manners — Honesty, Patience, and Forgiveness

As Al-Amin and Al-Sadiq, the Prophet (PBUH) was known for truthfulness in all dealings. For an IUT student, this means academic honesty: no plagiarism, no falsification of lab data, no copying in examinations. It means patience with difficult classmates and forgiveness of those who wrong you — not as weakness, but as deliberate practice of Prophetic character. The Prophet (PBUH) forgave those who stoned him at Ta'if; a student can forgive a friend who wronged them in a group project.

6. Being Good to Family

'The best of you is the one who is best to his family.' (Tirmidhi) For a student who may have left home for university, this practice means calling parents regularly, being respectful and kind in conversations with family, and not allowing the pressures of student life to make one irritable or dismissive at home. It means recognising that the private virtues one builds in family relationships — patience, service, respect — are the same virtues that produce excellent engineers and ethical professionals.

Conclusion: The six practices are not pious abstractions — they are a complete ethical curriculum. Prayer builds discipline and resilience. Seerah study builds wisdom. Salawat builds mindfulness. Generosity builds social conscience. Good manners build integrity. Good family conduct builds empathy. An IUT engineering student who practises all six does not merely love the Prophet (PBUH) in feeling — he or she becomes, in character, a living embodiment of the Prophetic model in the modern world.
